Transportation surrounds us in every facet of life. Pavements lead us to our supermarkets where we can fill up our hungry bellies. Road and train networks journey us across vast stretches of land to see every corner of our nation, and bikes and buses connect us to our family and friends after long days at work and school. We have all become familiar with and used to the luxuries of transportation. Now, as the world shifts towards more sustainable modes of transport, maybe it's time to see what we could improve.

Transportation has been explored from many different strands of literature, with each researcher's bias emphasising different aspects. As a group of multidisciplinary data scientists from various corners of the world who are bound together by a passion for transportation, we believe it's worth exploring the UK’s transportation characteristics to subsequently inform ways in which policies could be targeted to certain areas to alter transport modes. To understand this, we draw upon demographic factors are likely to influence transportation choice, and where similarities and differences are found within the UK. Total MSOA Travel

The UK is big, and when it comes to transportation, there is huge variation in accessibility and mode share within MSOA’s. What is an MSOA you ask? A Middle Layer Super Output Area (MSOA) is a geospatial statistic used in England and Wales. Created by the Office for National Statistics it is used to understand small areas of the country. The mean population of an MSOA is 7,200, with a minimum population of 5000. The 2011 census reports 7,201 MSOAs in England and Wales.

Our analysis shows within the average MSOA commute is dominated by car travel. 60% of people travelling to work are travelling by car, this is complemented by 74% of all households in the UK owning cars. The second most popular method for travelling to work is 10% of people who travel to work on foot (not accounting for those who work from home at 10.4%). This is then followed by bus, train and metro/underground/tram suggesting that public transport compromises around 15% of the journeys to work in England and Wales.

Discussion about travel types in cities

Let’s take London and plot the number of bus stops in each MSOA. Below is a histogram showing that variation. A what? A histogram shows the distribution of a certain variable; in our case it is count of Bus stops. The height of a bar shows the number of MSOAs that have a certain number of bus stops. For example, there are around 20 MSOAs that have 0-5 bus stops, and almost 250 MSOAs that have 15-20 bus stops (highest bar). Interestingly, there is a smaller number of MSOAs with over 150 bus stops!

So yes, London may be better served than any other area in the UK, but services in London are by no means uniformly distributed. Is it possible that some areas of London have worse service than other urban areas in the UK? The answer to that question, and many others, lies at the MSOA level.

Transport Access

What transport mode people choose to get to work can, of course, depend on access to different modes within the MSOA. In terms of stations, bus stops are by far the most common, with an average of 38.5 stops per MSOA. There are 277,094 bus stations across England and Wales. Only 1,811 out of 7,201 MSOAs have train stations, for a total of 2,305 train stations. An average MSOA has 0.32 train stations. This is even lower for metro, tram or underground stations, as only 535 MSOAs have these stations for a total of 899 stations in England and Wales. This means that the average MSOA has only 0.128 stations.

If there is significant variation in car usage then this should also show up in other modes of transport, as public transport may offer an appealing alternative. The averages for underground/tram/metro, train and bus usage is 3.4%, 4.7% and 7.3% respectively, and we can see that there is considerable variation across MSOAs. In the MSOAs with the lowest percentage use for each of these modes of transport is less than 1% which is associated with considerable reliance on private vehicle travel. However, some MSOAs show significant usage of each of these travel modes, with the largest for underground/tram/metro, train and bus usage being 55.7%, 46% and 45.5% respectively.
